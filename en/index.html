Maximiliansplatz – Your Starting Point in Bamberg

Welcome to Maximiliansplatz, the heart of Bamberg’s historic "Inselstadt" (Island City), surrounded by the scenic Regnitz River.

Did you know Bamberg’s Old Town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, boasting 2,400 half-timbered houses and centuries of history? Taste local specialties like the famous Bamberger Hörnla or the smoky Zwätschgabaamäs.

At Maximiliansplatz, don’t miss the historic Maximiliansbrunnen, a fountain celebrating King Maximilian I and other historical figures.

St. Martin's Church – A Baroque Masterpiece in Bamberg

Discover St. Martin's Church, also known as the Jesuit Church, a stunning example of Baroque architecture in the heart of Bamberg.

Built between 1686 and 1693, its magnificent façade by Leonhard Dientzenhofer and the breathtaking ceiling fresco by Giovanni Francesco Marchini make it a must-see.

Learn about its role in Bamberg’s religious and cultural history, and uncover more details about this Baroque treasure in the Bamberg City Tour App.

The Green Market – Bamberg’s Historic Marketplace

The Green Market, nestled in Bamberg’s Old Town, has been a hub of trade and social life since the Middle Ages.

Surrounded by historic buildings and Baroque facades, this vibrant square offers fresh produce, regional specialties, and a blend of tradition and modernity. Discover bustling market stalls alongside cozy cafés and shops that reflect the heart of Bamberg’s culture.

The Fork Man – Bamberg’s Unique Fountain

The story of "The Fork Man" (or Gabelmoo in Franconian dialect) is a fascinating part of Bamberg’s history. This iconic fountain was funded by Ludwig Krug, a Bamberg city councilman, whose legacy was brought back to the city in a smuggled salt wheel.

Commissioned in 1566 and featuring a statue of Neptune added in 1698, the fountain is affectionately named after Neptune’s trident, resembling a fork. Supplied by spring water from Stephansberg, it’s a true engineering marvel, with water flowing through wooden pipes across the city.

Want to uncover more about this quirky fountain and its mysterious history? Discover the full story in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Upper Bridge – Bamberg’s Historic Connection

The Upper Bridge, first mentioned in 1157, was the oldest link between Bamberg’s Mountain City and Island City. Rebuilt in stone in the 15th century, it later became a pedestrian zone in 1976.

Don’t miss the highlights: the crucifixion group from 1715, the statue of St. John of Nepomuk, and a stunning view of Klaus Dauven’s reverse graffiti on the Lower Bridge, perfectly contrasting the baroque Old Town Hall.

Discover the full history and secrets of the Upper Bridge in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Old Town Hall – Bamberg’s Historic Masterpiece

Legend has it that Bamberg's citizens built the Old Town Hall on an artificial island in the Regnitz River after the bishop refused to grant them land for it.

Admire the illusionistic frescoes that make the façade appear three-dimensional, and don’t miss the cherub’s leg that extends as a small sculpture. Inside, you’ll find the magnificent Rococo Hall and the Ludwig Collection of Porcelain.

Originally built in 1387 and remodeled in Gothic style in the 15th century, the Town Hall later underwent Baroque and Rococo renovations. The collection showcases vibrant Meissen porcelain and intricate Strasbourg faience.

Diocesan Museum – Bamberg’s Treasures of the Past

Located next to Bamberg Cathedral, the Diocesan Museum preserves a rich collection of art and history, including the imperial garments of Emperor Heinrich II and Empress Kunigunde.

Highlights include the exquisite Star Mantle, gold-embroidered robes, and unique papal vestments of Pope Clement II. Visitors can also admire Gothic sculptures, medieval textiles, and original figures from the cathedral portals in the cloister.

Discover Bamberg’s cultural heritage and step into history. For full details and insider tips, explore the Bamberg City Tour App.

Bamberg Cathedral – A Testament to History and Art

Bamberg Cathedral, commissioned by Emperor Heinrich II in 1002, stands as one of Bamberg’s most iconic landmarks. Combining late Romanesque and early Gothic styles, its four towering spires define the city’s skyline.

Inside, marvel at the famous Bamberg Horseman, Tilman Riemenschneider’s exquisite tomb of Emperor Heinrich II and Empress Kunigunde, and the only preserved papal tomb north of the Alps, that of Pope Clement II.

Explore this architectural masterpiece and uncover its treasures, from the Marian Altar by Veit Stoss to its stunning Romanesque details. Learn more about its rich history in the Bamberg City Tour App.

For additional information, visit Bamberg Cathedral on Wikipedia.

Prince’s Portal – A Masterpiece of Bamberg Cathedral

The Prince’s Portal is a stunning depiction of the Last Judgment, featuring Jesus as the Judge of the World. The tympanum above the door shows the faithful entering paradise on the right and the damned being taken away on the left.

Notable details include apostles standing on the shoulders of prophets and two symbolic female figures: Synagoga (Judaism) and Ecclesia (Christianity). While reflective of historical theological perspectives, they now stand as artifacts of their time.

Discover the full story behind this incredible portal and its intricate carvings in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Old Court – A Historic Jewel of Bamberg

The Old Court, once the residence of Bamberg’s bishops, forms a stunning ensemble alongside the Cathedral and New Residence. Its timber-framed buildings, dating back to 1475, and the Renaissance-style additions like the Beautiful Gate, showcase centuries of history and architecture.

Highlights include the relief on the Beautiful Gate, depicting the Virgin Mary, Saints, and the rivers Main and Regnitz. Today, the Old Court houses the Historical Museum and the Katharinen Chapel, a popular wedding venue. The courtyard also hosts the Calderon Festival each summer.

Step into history and discover more about the Old Court in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Historical Museum – Bamberg’s Cultural Treasure

Located in the Renaissance-style Old Court, Bamberg’s Historical Museum houses an impressive collection of art and artifacts, including works by Lucas Cranach and Pieter Brueghel the Younger.

Explore highlights like Franconian panel paintings, historical timepieces, and exhibitions on Jewish life in Bamberg. The museum also features temporary displays and special exhibits such as “A New Era Dawns”, showcasing Bamberg’s bourgeoisie during the 19th century.

Uncover more about Bamberg’s art and history by visiting the Historical Museum or diving into the Bamberg City Tour App.

New Residence – Splendor of Bamberg’s Prince-Bishops

The New Residence, former seat of Bamberg’s Prince-Bishops until 1802, features over 40 opulent rooms with stuccoed ceilings, exquisite tapestries, and Baroque furnishings.

Highlights include the magnificent Imperial Hall, adorned with frescoes by Melchior Steidl, the State Gallery showcasing masterpieces by Lucas Cranach and Pieter Brueghel the Younger, and the serene Rose Garden, home to 4,500 roses with breathtaking views of Bamberg.

Constructed between 1604 and 1703, the New Residence is part of Bamberg’s historic ensemble alongside the Cathedral and Old Court. Learn more about this remarkable landmark in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Rose Garden – A Blooming Oasis in Bamberg

Nestled behind the New Residence, the Rose Garden offers a tranquil escape with breathtaking views of Bamberg and the Jura hills.

Originally a Renaissance garden, it was transformed into a Baroque masterpiece in 1733 by Prince-Bishop Friedrich Karl von Schönborn and designed by Balthasar Neumann. The garden features 4,500 roses, framed by linden trees and adorned with sculptures by Ferdinand Tietz.

Relax in this idyllic setting or enjoy a treat at the café in the Rococo pavilion. Discover more about this picturesque garden in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Michaelsberg Monastery – A Historic Landmark in Bamberg

Founded in 1015 by Emperor Heinrich II, the Michaelsberg Monastery is renowned for its Baroque architecture and breathtaking views. Although the church is currently closed for renovations, the monastery remains a must-see.

Key highlights include the tomb of Saint Otto, the "Heavenly Garden" ceiling mural featuring 578 flowers and herbs, and the Michaelsberg Garden, which now produces Bamberg Silvaner wine.

Enjoy panoramic views from the terrace or explore the monastery's rich history and Baroque splendor. Learn more in the Bamberg City Tour App or visit the Wikipedia page for Michaelsberg Abbey.

Franconian Brewery Museum – Bamberg’s Brewing Heritage

Located in the historic vaults of the former Benedictine monastery brewery on Michelsberg, the Franconian Brewery Museum offers a unique insight into Franconia’s beer-brewing history. Established in 1979, the museum showcases over 1,300 artifacts across 1,000 square meters.

Highlights include exhibits on the brew house, malt house, fermentation and storage cellars, and tools used by brewers and maltsters. Visitors can also enjoy special exhibitions, such as artistically designed beer labels by Christian Krank and a monumental sculpture by Elias Frisch.

Complete your visit with a guided tour, Franconian beer tastings, and stunning views from the Michelsberg terrace. Discover more about Bamberg’s brewing tradition in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Little Venice – Bamberg’s Picturesque Fishing District

Nestled along the Regnitz River, Little Venice is a charming row of half-timbered houses from the Middle Ages, once home to Bamberg’s fishermen. These tightly clustered homes feature wooden balconies and tiny gardens, creating a storybook-like setting.

Explore this idyllic district with a scenic boat trip on the Regnitz, running from March to November, or visit during the annual Sandkerwa Festival in August, featuring the traditional "Fischerstechen" jousting event.

Discover more about this picturesque neighborhood, its rich history, and unique traditions in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Schlenkerla – Bamberg’s Iconic Smoked Beer

Schlenkerla is the most famous source of Bamberg’s unique Rauchbier (smoked beer). Brewed using traditional methods dating back to the Middle Ages, Schlenkerla continues the ancient practice of kilning malt over beechwood fires, giving the beer its distinctive smoky flavor.

The name "Schlenkerla" comes from a 19th-century brewer who walked with a limp, giving rise to this Franconian nickname. Today, the brewery produces its smoked beer in a 600-year-old rock cellar beneath Stephansberg.

Enjoy the rich, full-bodied taste of Schlenkerla Rauchbier, with its smoky aroma reminiscent of wood and Black Forest ham. Curious about Bamberg’s brewing history and the story behind Rauchbier? Discover more in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Empress Kunigunde – Bamberg’s Beloved Saint

Empress Kunigunde remains a central figure in Bamberg’s history. Canonized in 1200, she and her husband, Emperor Heinrich II, are the only imperial couple to achieve sainthood. Her popularity as a saint, especially in Bamberg, surpasses even that of Heinrich.

Legends surrounding Kunigunde include the Plowshare Trial, where she proved her innocence by walking barefoot over glowing plowshares, and the Penny Miracle, which showcased her justice and care during the construction of St. Stephen's Church.

Her legacy endures, with annual celebrations on her feast day and her statue on the Lower Bridge adorned with roses. Discover more about her fascinating life and miracles in the Bamberg City Tour App or visit her Wikipedia page.

At The Cranes – Bamberg’s Historic Port

At The Cranes, two historic iron cranes stand as reminders of Bamberg’s bustling port, where ships once loaded and unloaded their goods. Granted the staple right in 1156 by Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa, Bamberg became a key trade hub, with passing ships required to stop and offer their goods for three days.

This site offers more than history. Enjoy stunning views of Bamberg’s Old Town, the cathedral towers, and St. Michael’s Monastery. Nearby, the former slaughterhouse, now part of Bamberg University Library, adds to the charm of this historic area.

Today, The Cranes serves as a departure point for sightseeing boats. Discover more about its rich past in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Geyerswörth Palace – Bamberg’s Historic Gem

Geyerswörth Palace, originally owned by the Geyer family, became a ceremonial residence for Bamberg’s Prince-Bishops in the late 16th century. Remodeled by Erasmus Braun into a five-wing complex, the palace features the coat of arms of Prince-Bishop Ernst von Mengersdorf at its gate.

The palace has a dark history as the office of the infamous witch-hunter Prince-Bishop Johann Georg I during the 1620s, responsible for at least 600 executions. Despite suffering damage during the Thirty Years’ War, it was restored and now serves as a venue for city receptions and the annual Franconian Wine Festival.

While currently closed for restoration, visitors will soon be able to climb the palace tower for stunning views. Learn more about its rich history in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Villa Concordia – Bamberg’s Artists’ House

The Villa Concordia, established in 1997, is a Baroque water palace on the banks of the Regnitz River. It serves as an artists’ residence, offering annual fellowships to up to twelve visual artists, writers, and musicians from Germany and another selected country.

The villa hosts around 80 events each year, including exhibitions, readings, and concerts, most of which are free to the public. Fellows also benefit from collaborations and networking opportunities with other cultural institutions.

Discover more about this unique center for creativity and its historic setting in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Don Bosco Ferry – A Unique Bamberg Experience

The Don Bosco Ferry has been operating since 2012, connecting Lock 100 and Stephansberg. Run by the Don Bosco Youth Work organization, the ferry provides opportunities and support for disadvantaged youth.

Operating daily from April to October, this scenic ferry ride offers a unique way to explore Bamberg’s waterways while supporting a meaningful cause. Discover more about this special initiative in the Bamberg City Tour App.

Lock 100 – A Historic Engineering Marvel

Lock 100 marks the final lock of the historic Ludwig Main-Danube Canal, constructed between 1836 and 1845 under King Ludwig I. The canal connected the Main at Bamberg to the Danube at Kelheim, overcoming a 200-meter height difference with 100 locks.

Although short-lived due to competition from railways, the canal remains a testament to 19th-century engineering ambition. Discover its fascinating history and significance in the Bamberg City Tour App.
